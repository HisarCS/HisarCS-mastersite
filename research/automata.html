Automata

Six mechanisms, one shared base — and an AR layer that shows you the gears before you ever turn one.

Automata is a set of six open-source, 3D-printed mechanical kits — cams, cranks, and gear systems including a Geneva stop and a worm gear — built to close the gap between mechanical theory and hands-on practice for K-12 students. Every kit pairs with the team's own augmented-reality overlays, each one telling the story of an endangered marine species.

The idea

From FRC mentoring to a fabrication gap

The idea came out of mentoring the school's FIRST Tech Challenge and FIRST Robotics Competition teams — and noticing the same gap over and over. Newcomers to K-12 robotics labs understood mechanical systems in theory but struggled to apply that knowledge with their hands. Automata — Greek for "self-acting" — is the team's answer: mechanical devices designed to carry out a sequence of motion on their own, exactly the systems students see in daily life but rarely get to build.

Six kits, each isolating one core mechanical principle, fully designed in-house in Fusion 360 and 3D-printed on the FabLab's own farm of 20+ printers — completely open-sourced from day one, and currently working toward a patent.

Every kit shares the same base structure. Early versions were held together with screws and printed as many separate parts per kit — the team removed the screws for custom clips, then merged components and unified the base across all six kits, cutting filament waste and letting students focus on the mechanism instead of the assembly.
Six kits, six mechanisms

Same base, new physics each time

Each kit's AR overlay tells the story of a real endangered species — chosen so the mechanism and the conservation message reinforce each other.

Kit 1 — Cam-A

Rotation → linear oscillation

A contoured cam converts continuous rotation into controlled back-and-forth motion — the same principle behind engines and packing machines.

AR: manta ray
Kit 2 — Cam-B (Geneva Stop)

Continuous → intermittent motion

A pin engages slots on a driven wheel to convert steady rotation into precise, paused steps — used in clockwork, film projectors, and assembly lines.

AR: three jellyfish
Kit 3 — Crank

Rotation → reciprocation

A rotating arm and connecting rod turn circular motion into a controlled back-and-forth push and pull — core to engines, pumps, and presses.

AR: a shark
Kit 4 — Gear-A

Bevel gears, 90° redirection

Cone-shaped teeth mesh at an angle to redirect rotational motion between intersecting shafts — used in hand drills and automobiles.

AR: fish circling
Kit 5 — Gear-B

Shifting alignment, variable torque

Deliberately offset gears cause output speed and torque to fluctuate as they turn — a "weird gear" useful for non-uniform drive systems.

AR: two dolphins
Kit 6 — Gear-C

Worm gear, self-locking

A screw-like worm drives a toothed wheel at high reduction and can't be back-driven — the mechanism behind elevators and conveyor systems.

AR: a sea turtle
AutomatAR

Life below water, one gear at a time

Every AR overlay is built around the UN's 14th Sustainable Development Goal — Life Below Water. Point a phone at a kit and its motion is mirrored by a matching animation, tracked in real time with ArUco markers, with in-app text on what actually threatens that species.

Manta rays face overfishing and habitat loss; jellyfish are key to nutrient cycling; sharks keep ecosystems balanced by removing sick and weak animals; overfishing and netting shrink fish populations; the Yangtze River and Maui's dolphins are endangered by habitat loss and bycatch; and caretta-caretta sea turtles lose nesting beaches to coastal development. Every kit teaches a mechanism and a stake in the ocean at the same time.

Results

43% to 86%, in one workshop

An 8-question pre/post test — key components, mechanism purpose, how to use and customize the AR — run across 4 workshops with 83 students total.

Post-test

Accuracy in the team's final workshop — the clearest single result across all four. 78% of students scored more than half the questions correct after the session.

The largest jump showed up specifically among 5th and 6th graders — the group the team observed changing their rate of accuracy the most.

Design evolution

From screws, to clips, to a story

A year of trial and error: early kits were held together with screws across many separate pieces; the team moved to custom clips, merged components, and finally unified every kit onto a single shared base — before finding their way, through paper cutouts and origami, to full AR.

Partnering to reach earthquake-affected provinces

Working with the School Support Foundation (Okul Destek Derneği), the team is shipping kits and running online workshops toward all 81 provinces of Türkiye — with particular focus on regions still affected by the February 2023 Maraş earthquake. 18 of 81 provinces reached so far.

Going fully open-source

The team is building a public website with 3D design files, instructional videos, and step-by-step assembly guides, alongside Udemy lessons already reaching tens of thousands of students — removing the need for face-to-face teaching entirely.

A dedicated AR app is next

Planned: an interactive tutorial mode that lets students visualize gear and cam motion before physically assembling anything, plus large-scale controlled studies across multiple institutions to measure long-term retention.
